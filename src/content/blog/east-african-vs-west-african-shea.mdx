Most shea butter sold globally is sourced from West Africa - Ghana, Burkina Faso, Mali. But the shea tree also grows across East Africa, including Kenya, Uganda, South Sudan, and Ethiopia.

The two varieties come from different species of the same genus. They have different compositions and behave differently on the skin.

---

## The two species

The West African variety is Vitellaria paradoxa. It produces the familiar yellowish butter with a nutty scent. Most commercial shea butter globally comes from this species.

The East African variety is Vitellaria nilotica. It grows primarily in Northern Uganda, South Sudan, and southern Ethiopia. It produces a softer, more fluid butter that stays liquid or semi-liquid at room temperature. In Uganda it is traditionally called moo yaa - literally "the oil that doesn't harden."

---

## The composition difference

Vitellaria nilotica has a higher oleic acid content than Vitellaria paradoxa. Oleic acid is a monounsaturated fatty acid that penetrates the skin more readily than saturated fats. This is why Nilotica shea feels lighter and absorbs faster.

Vitellaria paradoxa is higher in stearic acid. Stearic acid has stronger occlusive properties. It sits on the surface and reduces moisture loss more effectively over time.

Neither profile is superior. They serve different purposes. Nilotica absorbs faster and feels lighter on the skin. Paradoxa lasts longer and provides a stronger moisture seal.

---

## The East African growing range

Vitellaria nilotica trees grow in a belt that stretches from northern Uganda through South Sudan and into southern Ethiopia. The range touches the Kenya-South Sudan border, though commercial harvesting in Kenya is minimal.

Most of the East African harvest is consumed locally or exported as raw kernels. There is very little processed Nilotica product available in mainstream Kenyan markets. This is changing slowly as awareness of the two species grows.

---

## Which one is Lovi

Lovi Shea Butter is sourced from West Africa (Vitellaria paradoxa). It is the denser, more occlusive variety. It suits barrier-heavy uses: post-wash sealing, the LOC method, and intensive care for dry areas like elbows and heels.

If you want the faster-absorbing texture of Nilotica shea, look for products specifically labelled with the species name. Most products labelled simply "shea butter" are paradoxa.

---

## Why it matters to know the difference

Ingredient labels don't distinguish between the two species. Both appear as Butyrospermum parkii or Vitellaria paradoxa on INCI labels, even though Nilotica is technically a different species.

If a shea product feels unusually light and liquid at room temperature, it may be Nilotica. If it is firm and dense, it is almost certainly paradoxa.

Neither is fake. They are genuinely different ingredients with different properties. Knowing which one you have helps you use it correctly.
