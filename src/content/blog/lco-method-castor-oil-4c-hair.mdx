Most people apply oil to their hair at the wrong step. They use it first, as a pre-treatment, or apply it directly to dry hair. Both approaches miss what the oil is actually for.

The LCO method - liquid, cream, oil - is the application sequence that makes castor oil work.

---

## What the letters mean

L is for liquid. Water, or a water-based leave-in conditioner. This is the moisture source. Everything else is built on top of it.

C is for cream. A moisturising cream, butter, or leave-in applied while the hair is still damp. This layer adds softness and additional hydration.

O is for oil. Applied last, the oil seals everything below it. It slows evaporation. The moisture from the liquid layer stays in the hair instead of escaping into the air.

---

## Why oil goes last

Oil is an occlusive. It does not add moisture. It reduces moisture loss.

If you apply oil to dry hair, it seals in the dryness. The hair feels coated but is not moisturised. This is why oiling routines often feel ineffective.

Applied while the hair is still damp, the oil seals in moisture that is already there. The result is different.

---

## Why castor oil works well as the oil layer

The oil layer needs to stay in place. Lighter oils evaporate or absorb faster. Castor oil is thick and viscous. It sits on the hair shaft longer, extending how long the moisture stays sealed in.

It also has one property that lighter sealing oils lack: ricinoleic acid, which has mild anti-inflammatory effects at the scalp. Applied as the seal step, it does double work.

---

## The practical routine

Step one: wet your hair or apply a leave-in conditioner to damp hair. Do not let it dry fully before moving on.

Step two: apply a small amount of cream or butter. Work it through in sections. The hair should still feel damp.

Step three: seal with a small amount of castor oil. Mix with a lighter oil if you want easier distribution. Jojoba or sunflower oil extends castor oil without reducing the seal.

Do not wait too long between steps. The moisture from step one is what you are sealing in. If the hair dries between steps, restart with a water mist.

---

## How often and how much

For most 4C hair in East Africa: apply LCO on wash day. Refresh with a water mist and a small amount of oil every two to three days between washes.

The region's climate is harder on moisture retention than most online hair content assumes. UV exposure, dust, and dry-season air pull moisture from the hair faster than temperate climates. Twice-a-week oil application is reasonable, not excessive.

Amount: less than expected. A five-shilling coin's worth of castor oil is enough for the full scalp. More does not seal better. It accumulates.

---

## The most common LCO mistake

Skipping the liquid step and going straight to cream and oil.

Without water or a leave-in first, the cream and oil layers have nothing to seal in. The routine feels heavy and ineffective.

The fix is always step one: get moisture into the hair first.

---

## LOC vs. LCO

Some people use LOC instead - liquid, oil, cream - with the oil applied before the cream. This works better for hair that finds castor oil too heavy as a final layer.

If LCO leaves your hair feeling weighed down, try LOC instead. Apply a lighter oil after the leave-in, then seal with a cream or butter on top.

Both sequences work. The key principle is the same: moisture first, seal last.
