There was no word for arthritis in most East African languages. There did not need to be. There were plants for it.

Castor oil for stiff joints after cold seasons. Bark extracts steeped in hot water for swollen knees. Warmed oil worked into the hands of elders who could no longer fully grip. The practitioners were the women of the household. The clinics were kitchens.

---

## What they were actually treating

Post-inflammatory joint pain. Arthritic degeneration. Gout, though they would not have called it that. Muscle soreness from physical labour.

These conditions existed long before clinical medicine named them. The treatments that worked spread through communities by observation. What helped was kept. What did not was dropped. Over many generations, this is a slow form of evidence. It is not a clinical trial. It is not nothing.

---

## Castor oil and joint tissue

The primary fatty acid in castor oil is ricinoleic acid. It makes up roughly 85 to 90 percent of the total fatty acid content.

Ricinoleic acid has documented anti-inflammatory properties. Studies from the early 2000s showed it inhibits the prostaglandin receptors that produce pain and swelling. This is the same mechanism that over-the-counter anti-inflammatory drugs use. The pathway was discovered by researchers. The function was already in use.

Topical application, warmed and massaged in, improves skin penetration and local circulation. Elders were doing this without knowing the pharmacology.

---

## What else was in the preparation

Castor oil was rarely used alone. It was blended with other plant materials.

Specific herbs varied by region and family. Some used eucalyptus leaf. Some used bark from the Prunus africana tree, native to highland East Africa. Some used combinations that have never been formally studied.

Prunus africana bark is now a commercially extracted ingredient for prostate conditions. Its traditional use included joint and muscle pain. The extraction industry found one application. Traditional medicine had found others.
