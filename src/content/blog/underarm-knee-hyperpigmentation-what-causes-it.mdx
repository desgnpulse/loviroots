This is one of the most searched skincare questions and one of the least answered honestly. Dark patches at the underarms, inner thighs, and knees affect the majority of people with dark skin. Most never mention it to a doctor.

Here is what causes it and what actually helps.

---

## Why it happens

The skin in these areas is different from the skin on your face or arms. It folds. It rubs. It is compressed throughout the day.

**Friction** is the primary driver. Skin that rubs against itself or clothing triggers low-grade inflammation. That inflammation produces pigmentation. The inner thighs and underarms experience friction almost continuously.

**Shaving and hair removal.** Each shaving stroke pulls slightly at the skin. That repeated micro-trauma inflames the follicle. The result is hyperpigmentation concentrated around each follicle opening.

**Hormonal influence.** The groin, underarms, and skin folds are sensitive to hormonal changes. Insulin resistance can cause acanthosis nigricans - thick, darkened skin concentrated in these areas. Pregnancy and certain contraceptives also affect pigmentation in skin folds.

**Deodorant ingredients.** Some deodorant formulations contain alcohol, fragrance, or aluminium compounds that irritate the skin with repeated use. Chronic irritation drives pigmentation.

---

## What helps

**Reduce friction.** Wear looser clothing where possible. Anti-chafe balms reduce mechanical irritation between skin surfaces.

**Switch hair removal methods.** Waxing, threading, or sugaring cause less repeated micro-trauma than shaving. If you prefer shaving, use a sharp single-blade with a fragrance-free shaving gel.

**Switch deodorant.** Look for fragrance-free, alcohol-free formulations. Natural crystal deodorants are less irritating for many people.

**Barrier support.** The same principle that helps the face applies here. Skin with a strong moisture barrier has a less reactive melanin response to minor friction and inflammation. Applying an occlusive after bathing reduces the ongoing irritation cycle.

**SPF on exposed areas.** Underarms are rarely exposed to sun, but inner knees and thighs sometimes are. UV compounds existing hyperpigmentation.

---

## What doesn't help

Strong bleaching products on already-sensitive skin. These areas are prone to contact dermatitis. Mercury and hydroquinone creams applied to folded skin are absorbed more rapidly than on flat surfaces. The risks are greater, not smaller.

Waiting for resolution without addressing the cause. The skin will continue to darken as long as friction and irritation continue.

---

Reducing the trigger usually matters more than the product you choose. Friction causes pigmentation. Address the friction and give the skin time. Most people see improvement within two to three months when the underlying cause is addressed.
