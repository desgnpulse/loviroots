Kenya has two dry seasons. The long dry runs from June through August. The short dry runs from January through March. Both create environmental conditions that are hard on the skin barrier in specific, predictable ways.

This is not the same skin problem that most skincare advice is written for.

---

## What dust season does to skin

Fine particulate matter settles on the skin surface throughout the day. In Nairobi and most Kenyan urban areas, this particulate is a mix of red clay dust, exhaust, and carbon particles.

Particulate on skin is not just a cosmetic issue. The fine particles mechanically abrade the top layer of the stratum corneum. Over repeated daily exposure, this degrades the lipid layer that holds the barrier together.

Simultaneously, low humidity draws moisture out of the skin faster than in wetter seasons. The barrier is being damaged from outside while moisture leaves from inside.

---

## Why the usual advice does not work here

Most skincare content is produced for audiences in the UK, US, and Northern Europe. The solution to dry skin in those contexts is often simply a heavier moisturiser.

That advice is incomplete for the East African context.

A heavier cream applied to dusty skin seals the particulate in rather than washing it off. The barrier is supported on top of an irritant, which increases the inflammatory response at the surface.

The correct sequence is different.

---

## The actual protocol for dust season

**Morning:** Rinse the face or body with clean water before applying anything. You are removing overnight particulate and sweat. Apply moisturiser to damp skin immediately after.

**After commuting:** Rinse again before applying anything to the face. The skin has been in traffic and air. Do not apply more product on top of what has accumulated.

**Evening:** Full cleanse before moisturising. Not just a rinse. This is the cleanse that actually matters. Remove particulate, sunscreen residue, and the day's buildup before sealing with an occlusive.

---

## What to use

Occlusives that work in a dusty environment need two properties. Light enough to not trap debris. Occlusive enough to seal once the skin is clean.

Unrefined shea butter works well here. It is not as thick as petroleum jelly and does not trap surface debris as aggressively. It provides the fatty acid support the barrier needs without heaviness that accumulates through a dusty day.

Apply at night as the primary moisturising step. In the morning, use a lighter application after rinsing.

---

## The SPF layer

Particulate from vehicle exhaust carries polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons. These compounds increase UV damage. The combined effect of particulate and Nairobi's UV levels is harder on skin than UV alone.

SPF in dust season is not optional. It prevents UV from compounding the barrier damage already caused by particulate exposure.

Apply SPF over your morning moisturiser. Let the moisturiser absorb for two minutes first. SPF applied over a still-wet butter will not distribute evenly.

---

## Internal hydration

During dry season, most people are mildly dehydrated without realising it.

The skin is the last organ to receive water. When the body is mildly dehydrated, water is directed toward vital organs and away from the skin. No topical product fully compensates for systemic dehydration.

Two litres of water a day is the standard minimum. In dry season, especially if active outdoors, the requirement is higher.

The topical routine and internal hydration work together. Neither is sufficient alone.
