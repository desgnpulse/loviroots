Kenya banned cosmetic skin lightening products in 2019. The products still cross the border. A Business Daily Africa investigation found mercury-containing creams sold openly in several Nairobi markets. Some were marketed to girls as young as twelve.

This article covers what these products actually contain and what they do to the skin over time.

---

## Mercury

Mercury is the most common active ingredient in unregulated lightening creams. It is absorbed through the skin and accumulates in the body. It suppresses melanin production by blocking the enzyme tyrosinase.

This effect reverses when you stop using the product. The suppression is temporary. The damage is not.

Long-term mercury exposure causes kidney damage, nerve damage, and skin thinning. In pregnant women it crosses the placenta. Products containing mercury don't list it plainly. On labels it appears as calomel, mercurous chloride, or ammoniated mercury.

---

## Hydroquinone

Hydroquinone is a regulated bleaching agent banned for cosmetic use in Kenya since 2019. It works by reducing melanin production and breaking down existing melanin clusters.

The problem is rebound. When you stop using hydroquinone, the skin often produces more melanin than before. This is called rebound hyperpigmentation. Some people develop ochronosis - a permanent bluish-black darkening that is very difficult to treat.

Hydroquinone also increases UV sensitivity. Used without sun protection, it accelerates the exact hyperpigmentation it was meant to treat.

---

## Corticosteroids

Topical steroids don't lighten skin directly. They suppress inflammation, and reduced inflammation reduces post-inflammatory pigmentation. Over time this creates an apparent brightening effect.

With extended use the skin gradually thins. The vascular layer beneath becomes visible - this is the shiny appearance associated with long-term cream use. Thin skin bruises easily, heals slowly, and is permanently more vulnerable to infection and sun damage.

---

## What to look for on a label

Some products list banned ingredients under unfamiliar names. Common ones:

- Mercury: calomel, mercurous chloride, ammoniated mercury, mercuric
- Hydroquinone: sometimes listed directly, sometimes absent entirely on unregulated products
- Steroids: names ending in -sone or -olone (betamethasone, clobetasol, triamcinolone)

If a product lists no active ingredients at all, that is itself a warning sign.

---

## What works instead

Addressing hyperpigmentation safely means identifying the cause. UV, inflammation, or hormones each require a different approach.

SPF prevents UV from darkening existing spots. Barrier repair reduces the inflammation that triggers pigmentation. Ingredients like niacinamide and azelaic acid reduce existing pigmentation without suppressing melanin production entirely.

None of these work fast. Safe results take longer than a tube of mercury cream. That is not a flaw in the approach. It is the point.
