Pregnancy changes the skin in ways that are rarely discussed honestly. The hormonal shifts involved are significant. The skin changes that follow are normal, predictable, and in most cases manageable.

This article covers what to expect and what works.

---

## What changes during pregnancy

Oestrogen and progesterone rise significantly during pregnancy. This stimulates melanin production throughout the body. For most pregnant women the result is visible pigmentation changes.

**The linea nigra.** A dark line that appears vertically on the abdomen. Present in most pregnancies. Fades naturally within months of delivery.

**Melasma.** Patchy darkening on the face - typically the cheeks, forehead, upper lip, and chin. Often called "the mask of pregnancy." More common in women with darker skin tones. It is triggered by elevated oestrogen combined with UV exposure.

**General darkening of existing marks.** Stretch marks, old acne scars, and other areas of hyperpigmentation tend to darken during pregnancy. Marks that were fading often become visible again.

---

## After delivery

Hormones stabilise gradually over weeks and months postpartum. As oestrogen returns to baseline, many pigmentation changes begin to fade on their own.

The timeline is slower than most people expect. Melasma can take six months to a year to fade significantly. This is normal. It is not a sign that the skin is not recovering.

---

## What slows recovery

UV exposure without protection. Melasma that is fading can be redarkened by sun exposure within days. This is the main reason postpartum skin often seems to not improve. The hormones may have settled. But UV keeps restarting the cycle.

Hormonal contraceptives. Some methods maintain elevated oestrogen. If melasma doesn't improve after delivery, the contraceptive method is worth reviewing with a doctor.

Breastfeeding extends some hormonal effects. The skin's pigmentation response may remain heightened during the breastfeeding period.

---

## What helps

**SPF, every morning.** Applied before going outside. This is the single most effective intervention for postpartum pigmentation.

**Barrier support.** Postpartum skin is often more reactive and more prone to dryness. A simple, non-irritating moisturiser applied after washing supports recovery. Unrefined shea butter is a practical option here - one ingredient, no fragrance, no preservatives, safe for use while breastfeeding.

**Time.** Postpartum skin changes are hormone-driven. They respond to hormone normalisation, not products. Products can protect the skin and support its function during recovery. They don't accelerate the underlying process.

---

## What to avoid

Aggressive brightening treatments while breastfeeding. Hydroquinone and retinoids are not recommended during breastfeeding. Mercury-containing products are dangerous regardless of stage.

Expecting fast results. Postpartum skin changes took nine months to develop. Reversal follows its own timeline.

---

The skin during and after pregnancy is behaving exactly as it is designed to. The changes are real. They are also reversible. Protect the barrier, block UV, and give it the time it needs.
