"Barrier care" has become one of the more discussed concepts in skincare. Most of that discussion centres on lighter skin types in cooler climates. Here is what it actually means for dark skin in an equatorial context.

---

## What the barrier is

Dead skin cells sit in a matrix of lipids - fats that seal the gaps between cells. This is the stratum corneum. Its job is to hold water in and keep irritants out.

When the lipid matrix is intact, the skin holds moisture throughout the day. When it is compromised, moisture escapes continuously and the skin becomes reactive to things that didn't previously bother it.

---

## How dark skin is different

Research on skin biology has historically focused on lighter skin types. What is known about darker skin is that the stratum corneum contains more melanin and has a different ceramide composition. Ceramides are a critical component of the lipid matrix.

Some research suggests darker skin types have lower baseline ceramide levels than lighter skin types. This is an active area of study.

What is clearer is that dark skin is more prone to post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation. When the barrier is breached and inflammation occurs, the pigmentation response is stronger. A stronger barrier means fewer breach events. Fewer breach events means less inflammation. Less inflammation means less ongoing hyperpigmentation.

---

## What compromises the barrier

Over-washing. Each wash strips some of the lipid layer. The skin can rebuild it - but if you wash more frequently than the skin can recover, the deficit accumulates.

Over-exfoliation. Chemical and physical exfoliants remove dead skin cells. Used too frequently, they strip the barrier before it has rebuilt.

Products high in alcohol or fragrance. Both are irritants. Repeated exposure builds a cycle of low-grade inflammation.

Equatorial UV. The Kenyan climate adds UV stress year-round. UV damages the lipid structure at the skin surface. SPF is not just sunburn prevention - it is barrier protection.

---

## What rebuilds it

Occlusive and emollient ingredients. Occlusives sit on the surface and slow moisture loss. Emollients fill the gaps between skin cells and make the barrier more flexible. Shea butter functions as both. Its fatty acid profile is compatible with skin lipids. It doesn't just coat the surface. It integrates into the existing barrier structure.

The principle is consistency over intensity. Two weeks of daily application after washing does more for barrier function than an occasional deep treatment. The skin needs time and regular input to rebuild what is missing.

---

## A simple protocol

Apply after washing, while the skin is still slightly damp. The dampness provides the water. The shea provides the seal. Let it absorb for a few minutes before getting dressed.

Morning and evening is enough. The results are cumulative. Most people notice a difference around the three-week mark. Not dramatic, but a consistent baseline moisture level. Reapplication becomes less frequent.
