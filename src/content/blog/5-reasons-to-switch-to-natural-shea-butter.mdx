Walk into any pharmacy in Nairobi and count the ingredients on the back of a moisturiser. Fifteen. Twenty. Sometimes more. Most of them are stabilisers, preservatives, and fillers keeping the other ingredients from separating in the bottle.

Here is what pure shea butter does instead.

---

## It actually feeds the skin barrier

Your skin is held together by lipids — fats that act as mortar between skin cells. When those lipids are depleted, the barrier leaks. Moisture escapes. Your skin feels tight by midday.

Shea butter is roughly 60% fatty acids, including a group called karite acids found almost nowhere else. These are structurally compatible with your skin's own lipids. They do not just coat the surface — they integrate into the barrier and reduce moisture loss across the day.

---

## There is nothing to react to

The most common cause of skincare reactions is fragrance, followed by preservatives and certain emulsifiers. These are standard ingredients in most moisturisers — including premium ones.

Pure shea butter has one ingredient: shea butter. If your skin tolerates natural fats, it will tolerate shea. That is why dermatologists recommend it for eczema-prone and sensitive skin.

---

## It works on skin, hair, nails, and lips

Most products are formulated for one surface. Shea butter does not care. The same jar works as a face moisturiser, a hair sealant for the LOC method, a cuticle treatment, and a lip balm.

One product. No extra shelf space.

---

## The results compound over time

Most moisturisers are maintenance. They keep skin feeling okay but do not change its baseline condition.

Shea butter used consistently — especially on slightly damp skin after washing — rebuilds barrier function over weeks. Customers who use it daily typically notice around the three-week mark that they are reaching for moisturiser less often. Their baseline moisture level has improved, not just their surface feel.

---

## It is honest skincare

Unrefined shea is yellow or grey-white. It smells faintly nutty. It is denser than a lotion and takes a few seconds to warm in the palms.

None of that is a flaw. It is what the ingredient looks like when nothing has been done to it.

The skincare industry has trained us to want products that feel like water and smell like flowers. That preference has nothing to do with results. Pure shea butter does less to your senses and more for your skin.
