Every skincare product sold in Kenya is required to list its ingredients. Most labels use INCI names - International Nomenclature of Cosmetic Ingredients. These are standardised Latin and chemical names that follow a consistent logic once you know the pattern.

Here is what to look for.

---

## How the list is structured

Ingredients are listed in descending order of concentration. The first ingredient is the largest component by weight. An ingredient listed tenth makes up a smaller proportion than one listed third.

Water (aqua) is usually first in most lotions and creams. It forms the base and can make up 50 to 80 percent of the product by weight.

---

## Ingredients worth recognising

**The plant-derived fats:** Look for names from known plants. Butyrospermum parkii is shea butter. Simmondsia chinensis is jojoba. Helianthus annuus is sunflower. These are generally beneficial for the skin barrier.

**Glycerin (glycerol):** A humectant. It draws water toward the skin. Usually beneficial at moderate concentrations. Without an occlusive layer on top, very high concentrations can pull water from the deeper skin in dry climates.

**Niacinamide (vitamin B3):** Well-studied. Reduces inflammation, supports barrier function, and reduces pigmentation over time. Generally safe at concentrations up to 10 percent.

---

## Ingredients to approach with care

**Fragrance (parfum):** The single most common cause of contact allergies in skincare. A single 'fragrance' entry can represent dozens of individual compounds. Most don't need to be disclosed individually.

**Alcohol (ethanol, alcohol denat):** Products that list alcohol early feel light and absorb quickly. Over time, that comes at the cost of barrier integrity. High-alcohol products are not appropriate as daily moisturisers.

**Preservatives:** Not all are problematic. Phenoxyethanol and ethylhexylglycerin are generally well-tolerated. The ones to be cautious about are formaldehyde-releasing preservatives - DMDM hydantoin, diazolidinyl urea, imidazolidinyl urea.

---

## The ingredients to avoid entirely

Mercury (listed as calomel, mercurous chloride, or ammoniated mercury) in any concentration. Banned in Kenya for cosmetic use. Still found in unregulated lightening products.

Hydroquinone at cosmetic concentrations. Also banned in Kenya for skin lightening. May appear unlabelled in unregulated products.

---

## A useful shortcut

A shorter ingredient list is not automatically better. But it is easier to evaluate. A product with five ingredients is easier to trace a reaction back to than one with twenty.

The simplest starting point is a single-ingredient product. You know exactly what you are using. If your skin tolerates it, you add one thing at a time.

Shea butter has one INCI name: Butyrospermum parkii. If you react to it, you know what caused it. That traceability matters more than most people realise.
