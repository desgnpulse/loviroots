Most infused oils on the market are herb-scented. The herbs sit in the oil long enough to colour it, then the product is packaged. The functional compounds that make the herb useful may or may not have transferred.

Comfort Oil is built differently. This is what is in it and why.

---

## The base: cold-pressed castor oil

Castor oil forms the base. Cold-pressed, not solvent-extracted.

The reason is ricinoleic acid. It makes up roughly 85 percent of castor oil's fatty acid profile. Applied topically with massage, it penetrates the skin and acts on local prostaglandin receptors that signal pain and swelling.

This is the functional foundation. Everything added to it extends or complements what the base already does.

---

## Eucalyptus

Eucalyptus leaf contains 1,8-cineole, also called eucalyptol. It is a natural analgesic and anti-inflammatory compound. Studies on topical eucalyptus show reductions in self-reported pain and inflammatory markers in knee arthritis patients.

The warming sensation eucalyptus creates when applied is not cosmetic. It is vasodilatory. Increased local blood flow means better delivery of the oil's compounds to tissue beneath.

Eucalyptus also has antimicrobial properties. In a product that sits on the skin without rinsing, this matters for product stability and skin safety.

---

## Ginger root

Gingerols and shogaols are the primary active compounds in ginger. Both are anti-inflammatory and analgesic.

Ginger has been studied for rheumatoid and osteoarthritic conditions. In several controlled trials, topical ginger reduced pain scores significantly compared to placebo. The mechanism involves inhibiting the same prostaglandin pathway as ibuprofen, though less potently.

Infusion into oil preserves the fat-soluble components. The water-soluble components transfer less completely. This is a limitation of oil infusion that we account for in concentration and infusion duration.

---

## Why heat matters for infusion

Extracting active compounds from plant material into oil requires time, heat, and the right plant-to-oil ratio. Cold infusion preserves some compounds better. Heat infusion extracts more completely but degrades heat-sensitive compounds.

Comfort Oil uses controlled low-heat infusion over an extended period. Not a fast cook. Not a cold steep. The method is chosen for the specific compounds being extracted from each herb.

---

## What to expect from use

Comfort Oil is not an immediate painkiller. It is not designed to replace pharmaceutical pain relief for acute conditions.

It is designed for daily management. Morning stiffness. Chronic low-level joint pain. Recovery after physical activity. Effects accumulate with consistent use over two to four weeks.

Apply to the affected joint. Massage for two to three minutes. The massage is part of the mechanism, not just a way to spread the oil. Movement drives penetration and stimulates local circulation.

Heat compounds the effect. Applying after a warm bath or shower, or covering the joint with a cloth after application, increases absorption.
