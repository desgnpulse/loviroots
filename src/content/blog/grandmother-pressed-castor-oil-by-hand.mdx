Before every bottle was manufactured, there were women who knew.

The castor plant grows wild across East Africa. It requires no cultivation, no irrigation. In rural communities it was simply there. The women of the household knew what to do with it.

The seeds were harvested, dried, and pressed. Not in a factory. In a kitchen, over heat, by hand. The oil that came out was thick, amber-coloured, and unlike anything sold in pharmacies today.

---

## What hand-pressing actually produces

Cold-pressing preserves ricinoleic acid, the compound that makes castor oil useful. It also preserves minor compounds that don't survive high-heat solvent extraction. Tocopherols. Flavonoids. Trace phytosterols.

Commercial castor oil is typically hexane-extracted at high heat. It is cheaper to produce at scale. It has a longer shelf life. It is also stripped of much of what made the original useful.

The oil our founder learned to prepare as a child was not like that.

---

## Why generational knowledge is specific

Her grandmother did not know the chemical name ricinoleic acid. She knew the oil worked for joints, for the scalp, for inflammation. She knew which seeds to pick. She knew to press it fresh, not to store it for months.

This is not a general claim that castor oil is good. It is specific knowledge about production, application, and concentration.

That specificity is what most modern products lack.

---

## The distance from plant to bottle

Most castor oil on the Kenyan market travels the same route. Seeds harvested in India or Brazil. Extracted using industrial solvents. Refined to strip colour and odour. Shipped, warehoused, labelled, and sold.

By the time it reaches the shelf, it is a standardised commodity. The connection to the plant and the knowledge behind it is gone.

Loviroots was built on the opposite logic. Less distance. Less processing. Closer to what the grandmothers actually made.

---

## Why this matters beyond nostalgia

Traditional preparation methods are not just heritage. They are, in many cases, a more direct path to the active compounds in the plant.

Research on ricinoleic acid supports anti-inflammatory effects on joint tissue and the scalp follicle. Traditional use preceded the research by generations. The grandmothers were not wrong. They were simply early.

This is the thread Loviroots tries to hold on to. Not the romantic idea of tradition. The actual knowledge inside it.
