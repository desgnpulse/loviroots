Dark skin produces more melanin. Melanin does absorb UV radiation. These two facts are often used to support a third claim: that dark skin does not need sun protection.

The third claim is wrong.

---

## What melanin actually does

Melanin is a pigment that sits in the skin's upper layers. It absorbs UV radiation and scatters it before it reaches the deeper layers of the dermis. This is a protective effect. It is real.

What it does not do is block UV. Even at the highest natural melanin levels, dark skin has a natural SPF equivalent of roughly 13. A standard SPF 30 product provides approximately three times more UV protection than that.

East Africa's UV conditions are demanding: high altitude, equatorial sun, outdoor work and daily commutes. At those exposure levels, a natural SPF of 13 is not sufficient.

---

## What unprotected UV exposure does over time

The effects are not always visible immediately. They accumulate.

UV exposure is the primary driver of post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation in dark skin. When the skin experiences micro-injury from UV, even without a visible burn, melanin production increases as a protective response. This is what makes dark spots darker and existing patches harder to fade.

UV also degrades collagen. Dark skin experiences this at a slower rate than lighter skin types, which is a genuine advantage. But it is not immunity. Long-term unprotected UV exposure produces the same underlying structural changes, just on a longer timeline.

---

## Why this myth persists

The no-sunscreen myth in dark skin communities is partly cultural and partly chemical.

Cultural: the message that dark skin "doesn't burn" is true enough in the short term to feel correct. Most dark-skinned people can spend extended time in the sun without the acute, visible burn that lighter skin experiences. This gets interpreted as protection. It is tolerance, not protection. The damage is happening at a level that does not produce immediate redness.

Chemical: traditional sunscreen formulations were developed primarily for lighter skin types. Many left a heavy white cast on dark skin that was aesthetically unusable. The reasonable response was to stop using them. The wrong conclusion was that sunscreen was not necessary.

Modern formulations have addressed the white cast problem. Tinted mineral SPFs and newer chemical filters are designed for a full range of skin tones. The old objection is less valid than it used to be.

---

## The connection to hyperpigmentation

Recurring dark spots or uneven tone get worse with unprotected UV exposure. It works against the goal, not toward it.

Hyperpigmentation treatment requires reducing inflammation and melanin overproduction. UV exposure triggers both. If the source of new melanin signals is not addressed, topical treatments have to work harder to maintain any progress.

SPF is not a cosmetic step. For hyperpigmentation-prone dark skin, it is the step that determines whether other interventions work or get reversed.

---

## A practical note for East Africa

The equatorial sun at altitude is more intense than temperate-zone sun. Nairobi, Kampala, and Addis Ababa all sit within a band where UV index regularly reaches moderate to high. Even on overcast days. Cloud cover does not block UV.

An SPF 30 applied every morning takes less than thirty seconds. It is the most effective single addition to any skincare routine for long-term evenness.

---

Melanin gives dark skin real advantages. It is not a substitute for protection. The two things are not in competition.
