Wildcrafted has become a marketing term. It appears on labels next to photographs of forests and promises of purity. Few brands using it explain what the word actually involves.

Here is what it means and why it is genuinely harder than farming.

---

## What wildcrafting is

Wildcrafting is the practice of harvesting plant material from its natural habitat, without cultivation.

The plant was not grown in rows. It was not irrigated, fertilised, or tended. It grew where conditions allowed it. The harvest is collected from that wild environment.

This is the original way humans gathered medicinal plants. It remains the only way to access plants that cannot be commercially farmed, or that only produce their useful compounds in a specific wild habitat.

---

## Why it is harder than farming

Farming controls variables. You plant in rows. You harvest at a predictable time. You scale by adding land.

Wildcrafting controls nothing. The plants grow when conditions allow. They are found where they choose to grow, not where you plant. Harvest depends on the season, the rainfall that year, and the specific plants available.

The people doing the harvest must know the plant well enough to identify it correctly, pick at the right stage of maturity, and take only what the population can sustain. Over-harvesting a wild plant population destroys the source.

This knowledge, which plant, which part, what stage, how much, takes years to develop. It cannot be automated.

---

## Why wild plants are sometimes different

Plants under stress produce more of certain compounds. This is a documented response called hormesis.

Wild plants face drought, insects, fungal pressure, and competition that farmed plants do not. In response, they produce higher concentrations of phenolic compounds, alkaloids, and other secondary metabolites. Some of these are the exact compounds that make the plant useful.

Farmed plants, grown in optimised conditions, may produce less of these compounds because the stressor that triggers their production is absent. This is why organic certification, which mainly addresses what chemicals are applied, does not tell you the same thing that wildcrafted origin does.

---

## The supply constraint

Wildcrafted ingredients are limited by what the wild population can provide. This is why some Loviroots products are seasonally out of stock.

It is not a logistics problem. It is a supply integrity decision. When the wild harvest is done for the season, we stop. We do not switch to a farmed or imported substitute.

This is less convenient for customers. It is also the thing that makes the product what it is.

---

## What to ask when a brand uses the word

Where was it collected? Who collected it? When was it harvested?

A brand that can answer those questions is probably using the word correctly. A brand that responds with "from nature" or "carefully sourced wild regions" is probably not.

The traceability is the thing.
